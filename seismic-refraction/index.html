We mapped a commercial site off Fenton Street last winter where the client was hitting refusal at 3 metres in what the driller thought was solid rock. Turned out to be a floating rhyolite boulder sitting on saturated pumice. That is Rotorua in a nutshell. The geothermal clays, the buried eruption sequences, the old lakebed sediments... nothing here is uniform. Standard borehole logs give you a point. Seismic tomography gives you the cross-section. We run refraction lines when the target is top-of-bedrock mapping for foundation design, and we switch to reflection processing when we need to image deeper layering or fault traces beneath the proposed pad. The array setup adapts to the site constraints: sledgehammer source for shallow investigations, weight-drop or accelerated drop when we need penetration past 30 metres. Data processing runs through iterative inversion routines that honour the curved-ray paths typical of Rotorua's high-velocity-contrast subsurface. Before you commit to a piling layout, pairing the tomography grid with an SPT drilling campaign at the anomaly locations gives you the ground truth that pure geophysics cannot deliver alone.

Seismic velocity is not a proxy for bearing capacity, but it is the fastest way to map where the bearing layer changes depth across a Rotorua site.

Technical details of the service in Rotorua

NZS 1170.5 requires site-specific subsoil class determination anywhere the default site class assumption would be unconservative, and Rotorua's shallow warm ground plus rapid lateral variability triggers that clause on almost every multi-storey project. Our refraction surveys measure P-wave velocities directly, and where we run surface-wave inversions alongside we derive Vs profiles to classify the site per NZS 1170.5 Section 3. The reflection method becomes essential when the velocity structure inverts, which happens frequently here because low-density pumice layers often sit beneath denser rhyolite flows. Standard refraction misses the hidden low-velocity zone entirely; reflection stack processing recovers it. We georeference every shot gather with RTK GPS tied to the NZTM2000 projection, so the final tomogram stitches directly into the civil model. In geothermal areas the warm ground can attenuate high-frequency signals, so we adjust the source energy and geophone spacing on the fly. For sites where the ash layers are thicker than 10 metres, we often recommend supplementing the seismic lines with a MASW survey to constrain the shear-wave velocity profile independently, because the two methods respond to different material properties and cross-validation reduces the interpretation uncertainty.

Critical ground factors in Rotorua

Rotorua sits inside the Taupo Volcanic Zone caldera complex, and the 1886 Tarawera eruption is a reminder that the ground beneath this city is geologically young and active. The main risk we encounter is not the rock itself but the variability: a perfectly competent ignimbrite shelf can pinch out over 20 metres of compressible diatomaceous lake silt within the footprint of a single building. Seismic refraction alone will not see the soft layer if it is velocity-inverted below a harder cap. That is the most common cause of foundation underperformance we have reviewed in this region. The second risk is hydrothermal alteration. Warm acidic fluids circulating through the Rotorua geothermal field chemically alter the rock matrix, reducing intact strength while the seismic velocity can remain deceptively normal. Reflection tomography helps here because the impedance contrast at the alteration front often produces a visible reflector. We always recommend at least one calibration borehole at the interpreted anomaly location, because the tomogram tells you where to look, not what the soil actually is.

Our services

Our seismic tomography work in Rotorua covers the full workflow from line layout design through to interpreted cross-sections. The two core methods we deploy are:

Seismic Refraction Tomography

Best suited for mapping top-of-bedrock depth and rippability across building pads up to 100 m wide. We run multiple overlapping spreads and invert with curved-ray algorithms that handle the high velocity contrasts typical of Rotorua volcanics. Deliverables include P-wave velocity sections, depth-to-bedrock contours, and NZS 1170.5 site class recommendations per Vs30 derived from joint inversion with surface-wave data where available.

Seismic Reflection Profiling

Applied when the target is deeper than 30–40 m or when a velocity inversion is suspected beneath the water table or within the geothermal reservoir. We use higher-energy sources and tighter CDP fold to resolve reflectors down to 150–200 m. This method is particularly useful for fault mapping, paleo-lake sediment thickness estimation, and imaging the base of the Taupo Pumice Formation prior to deep excavation design.

Questions and answers

How deep can seismic tomography see in Rotorua's geothermal ground?

With a sledgehammer source and a 48-channel spread at 5 m spacing, we routinely get P-wave first arrivals to 25–30 m depth in the drier rhyolite plateaus south of the city. In the lakebed sediments near the CBD, signal attenuation from warm ground and high moisture content can reduce that to 15–20 m. For deeper targets we use an accelerated weight drop or shotgun source, which pushes refraction penetration past 50 m and reflection imaging down to 150–200 m. The key variable here is not the equipment but the intrinsic attenuation of the near-surface pumice, which absorbs high-frequency energy very efficiently. We always run a test shot before committing to the full spread.

What does a seismic refraction survey cost for a typical Rotorua building site?

For a standard single-building-site investigation with three to four refraction lines totalling 200–300 lineal metres, budget between NZ$4.630 and NZ$8.990. The range reflects the number of spreads, the source type needed, and whether we run a parallel MASW line for Vs30 determination. Reflection profiling costs more per line because of the denser shot spacing and heavier processing. We provide a fixed-price proposal after a site walkover, because Rotorua access conditions vary enormously: a flat pumice paddock is straightforward, a bush-clad section on the lake edge is not.

Can seismic tomography detect the warm ground and hydrothermal alteration zones common in Rotorua?

Indirectly, yes. Hydrothermal alteration tends to reduce seismic velocity and increase attenuation, so the tomogram often shows a velocity low that correlates with the altered zone. However, the velocity reduction is not diagnostic by itself: a water-saturated pumice layer and a chemically altered rhyolite can have similar P-wave velocities. We flag these anomalies in the interpretation report and recommend targeted investigation, usually a borehole with core recovery through the suspect interval. The reflection method sometimes images the alteration front directly if the impedance contrast is sharp enough, which gives us a geometric constraint that refraction alone cannot provide.
